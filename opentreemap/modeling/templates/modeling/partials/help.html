Modeling Tree Growth and Benefits

What growth rates are used for estimating a tree’s size increase over thirty years?

OpenTreeMap uses growth equations based on information from the Urban Tree Database created by scientists at the U.S. Forest Service. The Urban Tree Database includes urban tree growth data collected from measurements of over 14,000 urban and park trees in seventeen cities over a fourteen-year period.

If sharing the modeling tree growth information displayed in OpenTreeMap, please use the following citation for the Urban Tree Database:

McPherson, E. Gregory; van Doorn, Natalie S.; Peper, Paula J. 2016. Urban tree database. Fort Collins, CO: Forest Service Research Data Archive. https://doi.org/10.2737/RDS-2016-0005

How do I add a species that is not currently on the list?

The species list is based on the species included for sixteen climate regions in the Urban Tree Database and reflects the species available in the database for the climate region in which the tree map is located. This list may differ from the species list available in the mapping portion of your tree map. Currently, there is no option to add other species to the list as growth equation information would not be available for those species.

How does OpenTreeMap calculate tree mortality?

OpenTreeMap applies a 5% annual mortality rate as a default, meaning that 5% of all trees added to the scenario will die each year. The 5% rate is based on research conducted by Dr. Lara Roman of the U.S. Forest Service and detailed in Trends in Street Tree Survival, Philadelphia, PA,, but users may adjust the overall annual mortality rate.

Custom mortality rates based on tree diameter and species are also available to accommodate locally specific mortality information (i.e. small maple trees die more frequently, etc). Users may enter mortality percentages that will apply to all trees in four diameter ranges or to a specific species in the same diameter ranges. Custom species and diameter rates replace the overall annual mortality rate.

Why is there an automatic replanting rate?

Tree planting contracts may include a provision requiring the contractor to replace any trees that die within a certain number of years after planting. The automatic replanting rate can be set to reflect that provision and ensure the growth model replaces any trees that die within the defined number of years since planting.

How are ecosystem benefits calculated over a thirty-year period?

Ecosystem benefits are calculated based on equations derived from the i-Tree Streets protocols developed by the U.S. Forest Service. The equations use the tree’s species, trunk diameter, and the climate region in which the tree is located to estimate the annual amount of energy conserved, stormwater filtered, air quality improved, and carbon dioxide removed by the tree. Economic values for ecosystem benefits are specified in the OpenTreeMap management console, with defaults based on contemporary prices.

How accurate are the ecosystem benefits?

The ecosystem benefits are estimates based on current research into tree growth and the environmental benefits associated with urban trees. More information about the i-Tree Streets protocols that influence the ecosystem is available from the U.S. Forest Service website.

Extending the benefits over a thirty-year period introduces the uncertainty inherent to any long-term model. Specifically, the currency values associated with ecosystem benefits are based on current pricing and not updated for inflation as the model runs. Projected ecosystem benefits should be viewed as general estimates rather than inerrant predictions.

What are the “Export Growth” and “Export Eco” options available in the scenario results?

The Export Growth and Export Eco buttons allow downloading CSV files which show the model's projected tree growth and ecosystem benefit values over a thirty-year period. These files may be useful for more extensive statistical analysis.
